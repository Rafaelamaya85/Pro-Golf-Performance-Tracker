Reflection

Building The Heat Index has been one of the most rewarding parts of this class because it connects directly to something I genuinely care about. As a golf enthusiast, I wanted to create a simple way of identifying the best golfers right now. The sport can be very complicated when it comes to navigating world golf rankings, interpreting analytics, and trying to figure out who is actually playing the best. Unlike most other sports, golf does not reset each season. Power rankings in sports like football or basketball focus on current performance, but golf rankings use a complex algorithm that includes results from the past two to three years. That makes it difficult to tell who is truly in form versus who is benefiting from a hot stretch years ago. This project was my attempt to simplify that.

One of the biggest decisions I made was to keep the formula straightforward. Instead of trying to replicate a complicated ranking system, I focused on a few key stats: wins, top 10 finishes, majors, and missed cuts. This helped make the rankings easy to understand and more approachable for a wider audience. I also designed the layout so users can quickly scan and understand the information without feeling overwhelmed. Features like the leaderboard cards, featured player section, and stat summaries were all intentional choices to keep the experience clean and simple.

The site evolved a lot from beginning to end. My initial Figma design was clear in concept, but once I started building it out, it presented a number of issues in terms of layout, spacing, and usability. As I tested the site with classmates and friends, I started to see where users hesitated or got confused. That feedback was really valuable and led to gradual improvements over time. Small changes like adjusting spacing, reorganizing sections, improving the visual hierarchy, and simplifying certain elements made a big difference. The version of the site now feels much more polished and easier to use compared to where it started.

A lot of what worked well came from that iteration process. Early versions of the site were functional but felt too basic. Over time, I added elements like the hero section, stat cards, and improved player cards to make the site feel more complete. The PGA and LPGA filter buttons added interactivity, and including recent tournament results helped make the rankings feel current and relevant instead of static.

There are definitely things I would do differently moving forward. If I had more time, I would focus on making the data dynamic instead of manually updating everything. Right now, updates require going into the code and changing values by hand, which is not ideal long term. I would also continue refining the mobile experience to make sure spacing and layout feel right on smaller screens. Adding more interactivity, such as search, sorting, or deeper player comparisons, would also improve the overall experience.

This project also felt personal in a different way. It took me back to when I was younger and wanted to work in sports. In a way, this project allowed me to revisit that interest while combining it with my IT experience and what I am studying now. I was able to use my education in technology and web development, apply the tools we’ve worked with throughout the CMPA program, and build something that feels meaningful. Something simple enough that my 11 year old could pull up, look at, and immediately understand.

One of the biggest things I learned through this project is the importance of iteration. The first version of my site was far from what it is now, and most of the improvements came from testing ideas, getting feedback, and making small adjustments over time. I also learned how impactful design decisions can be. Small changes like spacing, visual hierarchy, and highlighting key information can completely change how a user experiences the site.

Overall, this project helped me understand how to take a personal idea and turn it into a functional, userfocused product. It reinforced that simple, clear solutions can often be more effective than complex ones, especially when the goal is to make something accessible and easy to use.
