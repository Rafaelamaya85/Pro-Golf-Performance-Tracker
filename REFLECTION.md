Reflection

# REFLECTION.md 

Building The Heat Index was a lot of fun and incredibly rewarding because it connects to something I genuinely care about. As a golf obsessed fan, I wanted to create a simple way to identify the best golfers right now. Golf in general is a complex sport that can be overwhelming. The rankings are often difficult to understand because they rely on complex systems that include results from the past two to three years. Unlike other sports that focus on current performance, golf can make it hard to see who is actually in form and not benefitting from a hot streak from a few years ago. This project was my attempt to simplify that and make it easier for someone to quickly understand who is playing the best at any given moment.

One of my main decisions was to keep the formula straightforward by focusing on wins, top 10 finishes, majors, and missed cuts. This made the rankings easier to understand and more accessible. I also designed the layout to be clean and easy to scan, using elements like leaderboard cards, a featured player section, and stat summaries to guide the user. I wanted the site to feel simple and intuitive, where someone could land on the homepage and immediately understand what they were looking at without needing to read a long explanation first.

The site evolved a lot from beginning to end. My initial Figma design was clear, but once I started building it, I ran into layout and usability issues. The code just did not transfer as I expected so I had to adjust. Testing with peers and friends helped me identify problem areas and improve the site over time. I started to notice where users hesitated or where things were not as clear as I thought they were. Small adjustments to spacing, organization, and visual hierarchy made a big difference, and the final version feels much more polished and user friendly compared to where it started.

Another thing that worked well was the iteration process itself. Instead of trying to build everything perfectly the first time, I made small improvements each step of the way. Adding features like the hero section, stat cards, movement indicators, and clearer leaderboard layouts helped the site feel more complete and professional. These improvements were guided by feedback and by seeing how the site actually functioned in real use rather than just how it looked in design.

Small changes to the different iterations helped me evolve the page in a manageable way that greatly improved usability and overall experience. I became more comfortable using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript together, and I started to better understand how layout, spacing, and visual hierarchy affect how users interact with a site.

If I had more time, I would look to make the data dynamic instead of manually updating it as the more the site grows the more time consuming it can be to update each individual section and player. I would also look to improve the mobile experience further, and add features like search or player comparisons as well. These additions would make the site more interactive and useful, especially as more data is added over time.

Overall, this project helped me turn a personal pet peeve about golf into an exciting functional product. It also allowed me to combine my interest in sports, my IT experience, and web development skills from the CMPA program to create something practical and easy to use. The end goal was to build something simple enough that even my 11 year old could pull it up, look at it, and immediately understand who is playing the best right now.
